In Between Generations

April 6, 2025

People see the surface: The job title. The steady income. The smile I’ve learned to wear without effort. “He’s doing well,” they say. And move on.

What they don’t see: The rent I send away each month—clean, on time— knowing it builds nothing that lasts. The cost of distance from home, measured in flights I delay and voices I miss. The 2AM budgeting sessions, trying to stretch one life across three timelines.

My wife is twenty-three. Her friends post weekend escapes, careless adventures. She doesn’t ask, but I see the pauses the way her scrolling slows on someone else’s joy. She traded in her youth for a quieter kind of life. And I carry the guilt of that trade, quietly.

Every time I save for our daughter, it’s a moment I can’t give her mother now. Each “maybe next month” is a little piece of living we don’t get back.

Meanwhile, my parents’ world is thinning. A home that creaks. A car that coughs. Savings that shrink in the shadow of rising costs. They don’t complain, but I can feel the weight in their words.

If they spend now, what happens later? If they don’t, what slips through the cracks today? If I help, what do I take from my child? If I don’t, what kind of son am I?

This is the math I do that no one sees. The kind that doesn’t fit in spreadsheets. The kind that makes you feel like you’re always behind no matter how fast you move.

I make okay money, sure. But there’s never enough to secure three generations at once. My wife’s fleeting youth. My daughter’s uncertain future. My parents’ vulnerable present.
